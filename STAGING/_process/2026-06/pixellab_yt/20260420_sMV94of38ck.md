# I Tried Using PixelLab for a Game Jam. Here's What Happened

I joined a game jam with one very specific theme, horses. The twist was that the whole thing had to work with one-handed controls. A few days later, I had a playable card game prototype, a full custom deck, animated race horses, and somehow we placed sixth. So, for this jam, I knew I had to move fast. Not just prototype [music] fast, not just placeholder fast. I wanted to see how far I could push Pixel Lab [music] as the actual asset pipeline for the game. And because this was going to be a card game, the first thing I needed was the deck. So, I opened A Sprite, made a 64 by 64 canvas, and jumped [music] into Create Image Pro, small to XL. I started simple, just retro card shapes. I wanted a base. The first results were decent, not terrible, but not very interesting either. [music] So, I started pushing the prompt more, better designs, more detail, more texture. At this point, I wasn't trying to get perfect final art. I was trying to get direction. I just needed something that felt like the beginning of a real visual style. Once I had a few cards I liked, I cleaned them up a bit and brought them into Create Image from Style Reference Pro. And that's where things started getting a lot better. I added those cards as [music] style references. Then I went into Low Spec and picked the CSC 16 palette. I liked it because the colors felt muted and controlled, [music] which immediately made the cards feel a little more intentional. So, with those style references and that palette, I started generating the heart suit. And honestly, even then, I still wasn't fully sold. The cards were fine, they just felt kind of [music] plain. So, I kept iterating. I added things like nicely textured cards and cool textures [music] to try to force more personality into the results. That helped, but the real breakthrough came when I added one [music] phrase, horse-themed. That was the shift. All of a sudden, the cards had identity. They felt more specific, more weird, more memorable. It stopped feeling like generic playing cards and started feeling like the start of an actual game. The only problem was that some of them leaned a little too cute, [music] and that wasn't really the vibe I wanted. So, I pushed the prompt again and started asking for humanoid horses, especially for the higher value cards. And that was the moment where it really clicked. Now the deck had character. Now it had a hook. Now I could actually see the game. Once I had a few strong results, I started removing the backgrounds manually. And this is one of those small workflow details that [music] matters a lot. With cards, automatic background removal can get messy because the model might treat the inside of the card like background. So, for this kind of asset, manual cleanup was just the safer move. Then I took those cleaned up cards [music] and fed them back in as the new style references. So, the next generations would be guided by the stronger versions instead [music] of the rough early ones. That loop is honestly one of my favorite parts of working this way. Generate, choose what works, clean it up, feed it back [music] in, generate again. Each pass gets tighter. So, once I had a tile of heart cards I really liked, I shifted into cleanup mode. I fixed outlines, adjusted details, cleaned up the heart symbols, copied and pasted shapes where needed, and made sure everything felt cohesive. And this is where I think Pixel Lab is the most useful. Not as a magic button that instantly gives you final art, but as a tool that gets you somewhere good faster. It helps you get past [music] the blank canvas. It helps you find the style sooner. And then your manual work becomes polish instead of [music] guesswork. And for a game jam, that matters a lot because usually you don't lose time on one huge mistake, you lose it in tiny redraws. Damn it, WHY IS IT SO HARD? ANOTHER FAILURE. SO, once the hearts were in a good place, I saved them into the project folder and started organizing everything properly. Assets folder, cards folder, hearts folder. Not exciting, but very necessary. Then I used those cleaned up [music] cards as the main style references and moved on to the other suits, spades, diamonds, clubs. Same workflow, same idea. At one point, I got these medieval horse characters that honestly looked kind of amazing, but every suit still needed cleanup. Symbols had to match, shapes had to be consistent, details had to be unified so the full deck felt like one thing instead of four separate experiments. That part is not glamorous, but it's important. It's the difference between something that feels generated and something that actually feels designed. And yeah, I know this is maybe a slightly strange way to tackle a jam. Normally, [music] you'd prototype first and make the pretty assets later. But I wanted enough of the deck done early that once the [music] prototype worked, it would already feel like a real game. So, once I had enough cards built, I put together a small prototype. And seeing the deck actually working [music] in the game was the first big moment of relief because before that, they were just assets. Now they had a role. Now they had context. Once the main deck was working, [music] I moved on to one of my favorite parts of the whole project, the jokers. At first, I used the cards I had already made as style references and generated broader joker variations, different horse jokers, different poses, different styles, different colors, just exploring. [music] And some of those were really cool, but I didn't want the jokers to feel random. I wanted them to matter. So, instead of prompting vague joker ideas, I made a list of the jokers I wanted and what each one was supposed to do. That gave the whole process way more direction. Now I wasn't just prompting make me some jokers, now I was prompting specific gameplay identities. And for a lot of those, I switched workflows and used inpainting. [music] I took a simpler card, brought it into inpainting, masked the center so the border stayed intact, and then generated directly inside the card frame. That worked really well because it kept the card proportions [music] and layout consistent while letting me experiment with the art inside. So, I do prompts [music] for things like a flat shaded joker card, lucky horseshoe, horse joker, limited color palette with the word joker on the upper and lower side like a real playing card. That specificity helped a lot. And yes, sometimes the text [music] came out messy. That's normal. But if the art itself looked good, I kept it and fixed the text manually. That's a big part of the whole workflow here. You don't throw away a strong generation just because one detail is broken. You keep what works. You edit what doesn't. [music] You keep moving. One of the jokers I made was based on a photo finish idea, like a horse barely winning the race. That one came out especially cool, but again, the text needed cleanup. [music] So, I fixed it instead of rerolling forever. And once I started prompting the actual names of the jokers instead of generic joker ideas, the results got much better. The AI had a clearer target, and the [music] cards started feeling much more intentional. So, I repeated that workflow across the whole joker list. Generate, select, clean, refine, repeat. And by the end of it, I had a full set of custom jokers that matched the deck and also had actual [music] gameplay purpose. Then I added the joker logic into the prototype. And that's when the project started [music] feeling like a real game instead of just a visual experiment because now it had systems. It had rules. [music] It had identity. It had special cards that actually meant something. But at that point, we still didn't have the most important part of a horse-themed game, the horses. So, I went back to the racetrack background and used it to estimate how large [music] the horses should be on screen. Then I opened a new 64 by 64 canvas and started generating side scroller horses. The first prompt was basically horse for video game, idle pose, looking east, retro style, side scroller. And I got some nice looking horses. The problem was scale. They were too big. So, I resized one of them, used that smaller version as a style reference, [music] and prompted again. I also added fully to the side so the horse would read more clearly in profile instead of turning toward [music] the camera. That helped a lot. Then I switched over to the Pixel Lab website because it was easier to iterate there. I went back into Create Image Pro and [music] then Create from Style Reference and kept building out more variations. I prompted different types of horses, idle pose, looking east, side scroller. Same basic structure, just trying to get more options that actually fit the scene. The next batch was still a little large, so I shrank them again, used that as the new style reference, and generated one more time. And there we go. Now the horses actually fit. Then I added flat shaded to the prompt, and that really helped lock in the look. At that point, I loved the results. So, I cleaned [music] them up and removed stray pixels. Then I had a very obvious thought. These are race horses. They should probably have jockeys. So, I prompted race horses with race jockeys, [music] looking east, side scroller, flat shaded. And that gave me exactly what I wanted, different horses, different jockey outfits, enough variation that the race started feeling much more alive. [music] Then came the next big step, animation. So, I took the horses into Animate with Text Pro and prompted something like [music] the horse is running, racing, legs crossing. And just to be clear, this happened before Animate with Text V3 [music] existed. So, this was the earlier version of the tool, but even then it was enough to get the motion started. [music] And that was one of the most satisfying moments in the whole project because that was the point where the game stopped feeling like a card prototype with horse art and started feeling like an actual [music] horse racing game. Of course, the animation still needed cleanup. So, I spent more time editing, fixing, and polishing the frames [music] to make them work better once they were actually in game. Then I tested them in the race scene with the jockeys, the horses, and the CRT effect I had added on top. And that part made the whole thing feel way more alive. From there, I kept cleaning up the animations, fixing details, and making sure the [music] motion felt coherent once it was all moving together. Then I went back and made more specific cards for certain jokers, just adding [music] extra custom details where they were needed. So, by that point, the project had the deck, the jokers, the race horses, the jockeys, the animation, and the gameplay, but it still needed presentation. It needed a name. So, I went back to the creator page and opened create image pro again to make a logo. I prompted a blackjack and horse themed text logo saying "Derby 21, retro minimalistic" [music] on a 256 by 256 canvas. I found one I liked, removed the background, cleaned it up, [music] and used it for the start page. And that made a huge difference because suddenly it didn't just feel like a jam prototype. It felt like an actual game with an identity. But we were still missing one more background. The menu where you choose your horses. [music] So, I went back into create image pro and prompted a background of vintage parchment paper with subtle faded horseshoe watermarks and a sepia-toned texture. I used a 344 by 192 canvas, basically a 16 by 9 layout. And the first result was pretty good, but still too busy. So, I brought it into edit image pro and simplified [music] it. Less detail, simpler paper texture, subtle horseshoe watermarks, divided in the middle like a book with a crease down the center. That gave me something much cleaner and much more usable as a menu screen. [music] Then I did even more cleanup on the animations, and finally there was one last thing missing, the [music] ending screens. If the player wins, what do they see? If the player loses, what do they see? So, I went back into create image pro one more time and prompted a background image of a horse winning money, horse [music] betting, flat shaded, single line outlines. I generated a few options until I found one I liked. Then I prompted the losing version, too. And honestly, that one came out pretty funny. At that point, the game finally had a beginning, a middle, and an end. Then to wrap everything up, I added some sound effects in 11 Labs and used Suno for the music. I slowed the music down a bit, added some texture, and tried to make it fit the game better overall. And the game itself was built in Love 2D with antigravity.
